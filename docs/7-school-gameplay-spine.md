# Void Wars: Oblivion — 7-School Gameplay Spine

## Role Summary

This document folds the locked 7-school canon into the playable structure of *Void Wars: Oblivion* without introducing a giant one-shot system rewrite.

- **Macro powers:** the three empire layers remain the top-level strategic powers that shape gear paths, upgrade identity, and broad territory control.
  - **Bio Empire**: mutation, flesh adaptation, predatory resilience.
  - **Mecha Empire**: armor, automation, heat discipline, frame efficiency.
  - **Pure/Rune Empire**: ritual throughput, warding, conversion, resonance control.
- **Regional doctrines:** the seven schools become the major front doctrines that determine how a region behaves in survival, crafting, route selection, and boss progression.
- **Sin pressure identities:** each school’s sin becomes the region’s gameplay pressure, meaning the player is not just told what the doctrine is — they feel it through attrition, scarcity, temptation, overextension, and timing windows.
- **Current-project fit:** this structure layers naturally on top of the project’s existing exploration, hunting, crafting, inventory, recovery, and faction-path direction instead of replacing them.

## How Each School Changes Survival

### 1. Norway — Wrath
- The region punishes stationary play.
- Threat escalation rises the longer the player stays in one combat pocket.
- Survival becomes a chase-management problem rather than a pure damage race.
- Existing condition systems can absorb this by treating Wrath zones as places where failed disengages create repeated recovery pressure.

### 2. Peru — Gluttony
- The region drains active readiness by consuming supplies, charges, or temporary effects.
- Survival becomes about deciding what to feed the region and what to preserve.
- The doctrine fits current inventory and reward loops because the danger comes from over-consuming your own stockpile.

### 3. Greece — Envy
- The region learns from repeated player behavior.
- Survival pressure increases when the player leans too hard on one weapon pattern, mutation route, or timing habit.
- The core survival lesson is adaptation under observation.

### 4. Lebanon — Lust
- The region offers seductive short-term power to create delayed instability.
- Survival pressure comes from choosing whether an immediate heal, damage boost, or shortcut is worth later corruption or condition loss.
- This keeps the school active in minute-to-minute play instead of just cutscene flavor.

### 5. India — Greed
- The region punishes players for carrying too much value too greedily.
- Survival becomes logistical: when to extract, when to bank, and when to leave treasure behind.
- This is a strong bridge between exploration, inventory, and eventual market/crafting loops.

### 6. Egypt — Pride
- The region attacks anything exposed, visible, and unprepared.
- Survival pressure is created by sightlines, marks, and high-discipline ranged punishment.
- It naturally pushes route discipline, cover usage, and anti-detection planning.

### 7. China — Sloth
- The region tries to make the player late.
- Survival pressure comes from slowdown, automation buildup, and delayed consequences that become fatal if not interrupted early.
- This works well as a “soft fail snowball” doctrine for missions and strongholds.

## How Each School Changes Crafting

The schools should influence crafting through **countermeasure style**, not through seven separate crafting systems.

### Shared rule
Each doctrine biases what players want to craft before entering it. That means the existing crafting direction can stay intact while school identity changes demand, recipe value, and prep behavior.

### Countermeasure styles by school
- **Wrath / Norway:** burst sustain and disengage tools. Recipes should favor emergency healing, bleed control, sprint restoration, and pursuit breakers.
- **Gluttony / Peru:** efficiency and conversion tools. Recipes should turn excess materials into stabilized utility so the region cannot simply eat the player’s economy.
- **Envy / Greece:** modular swap tools. Recipes should reward alternate loadouts, adaptation kits, and anti-copy consumables that scramble pattern recognition.
- **Lust / Lebanon:** cleansing and temptation-management tools. Recipes should neutralize corruption, shorten instability windows, or safely cash out forbidden boons.
- **Greed / India:** compression and asset protection. Recipes should reduce carry burden, protect stored value, and convert scattered loot into dense high-value forms.
- **Pride / Egypt:** shielding and anti-exposure tech. Recipes should bias armor plating, reflectors, signature dampeners, and anti-mark components.
- **Sloth / China:** tempo restoration. Recipes should reduce slow effects, refresh action tempo, interrupt machine cycles, or bypass queue penalties.

### Why this fits the current project
- It gives immediate meaning to existing crafting and inventory direction.
- It avoids introducing a new profession tree per school.
- It supports future profession depth by making crafters valuable because they prepare doctrine-specific counters.

## How Each School Changes War Map Play

The war map should read the three empires as the macro-political layer and the seven schools as the regional doctrine layer.

### Macro layer: empire control
- **Bio** fronts feel alive, mutating, and pressure-heavy.
- **Mecha** fronts feel territorial, disciplined, and positional.
- **Pure/Rune** fronts feel ritualized, extractive, and conversion-driven.

### Regional layer: doctrine behavior
Each front or citadel inherits one doctrine that changes how that node plays on the map.

- **Wrath nodes** escalate if ignored after contact. They are ideal “threat snowball” regions that force decisive action.
- **Gluttony nodes** consume nearby value streams. They can raise mission upkeep, tribute costs, or extraction loss until answered.
- **Envy nodes** react to successful player patterns. Repeating the same route or build into them becomes less effective over time.
- **Lust nodes** offer tempting shortcuts, high rewards, or unstable treaties that create future complications.
- **Greed nodes** become high-value but high-tax targets. They attract players because of loot density while punishing indecision.
- **Pride nodes** project control into adjacent territory through visibility, range, and deterrence.
- **Sloth nodes** delay nearby operations with cooldown drag, travel friction, or deployment lag.

### Resulting map behavior
- The same empire can feel different across multiple fronts because doctrine changes the actual gameplay pressure.
- The player gains a practical reason to care which citadel they attack first.
- Front selection becomes a survival/crafting decision, not just a story choice.

## Best First Implementation Slice

The first slice should be light, structural, and compatible with the project’s current state.

### Recommended slice
1. **Add school doctrine data as structured content** for seven fronts.
2. **Tag exploration and hunting missions with one doctrine** instead of building a whole new world map.
3. **Apply one survival modifier and one resource bias per doctrine** in mission generation/rewards.
4. **Expose one recommended crafting countermeasure** in mission prep or guidance UI.
5. **Gate one elite/boss encounter with a doctrine-specific breakthrough rule** described in mission text.

### Concrete first-pass examples
- **Wrath mission modifier:** repeated encounters raise incoming damage or pursuit chance.
- **Gluttony mission modifier:** completion drains one extra consumable or temporary buff slot unless a countermeasure is equipped.
- **Pride mission modifier:** open-area encounters apply a mark unless broken by cover or a crafted shield tool.
- **Sloth mission modifier:** timers and cooldowns recover more slowly unless interrupted via a crafted reset device.

### Why this is the best first slice
- It uses systems already implied by exploration, inventory, condition, rewards, and crafting.
- It makes the seven schools mechanically legible immediately.
- It avoids shipping a giant front-control or procedural-region feature too early.
- It creates a scalable template for future war-map and citadel content.

## Final Result

If implemented this way, the game structure becomes:

- **Empires define macro identity**: Bio, Mecha, and Pure/Rune remain the top-level power blocs.
- **Schools define frontline behavior**: every major region, front, or citadel plays differently because doctrine changes pressure, rewards, and preparation.
- **Sins define felt gameplay pressure**: the player experiences Wrath, Greed, Pride, and the others as active mechanical stressors.
- **Crafting gains strategic purpose**: preparation becomes doctrine-aware rather than generic stat inflation.
- **The war map gains meaningful route choice**: picking a front becomes choosing what kind of survival problem to solve.
- **The project stays on-course**: the canon strengthens current exploration/crafting/survival direction instead of demanding a total redesign.
